\exercise{Use an $\epsilon-\delta$ proof to show that 

$\ds \lim_{x\to 1} 5x-2=3$.
}{Let $\epsilon >0$ be given. We wish to find $\delta >0$ such that when $\abs{x-1}<\delta$, $\abs{f(x)-3}<\epsilon$. 

Scratch-Work:
Consider $\abs{f(x)-3}<\epsilon$, keeping in  mind we want to make a statement about $\abs{x-1}$:
\begin{align*}
\abs{f(x)-3}&<\epsilon \\
\abs{5x-2-3}&<\epsilon \\
\abs{5x-5}&<\epsilon\\
5\abs{x-1}&<\epsilon \\
\abs{x-1}&<\frac\epsilon5\\
\end{align*}
suggesting $\delta =\frac\epsilon5$.

Proof: Given $\epsilon>0$, let $\delta =\dfrac\epsilon5$. Then:
\begin{align*}
\abs{x-1}&< \delta \\
\abs{x-1}&< \frac\epsilon5 \\
5\abs{x-1}&< \frac\epsilon5\cdot 5\\
\abs{5x-5}&< \epsilon \\
\abs{5x-2-3}&< \epsilon  \\
\end{align*}
Thus  $\ds \lim_{x\to 1}5x-2=3$.}
